Do you know what the flecks on the windshield edges and the tempered edges are for and why are they black?

By David Pesquera - Director, EuropeCorporate Supply Chain & Purch

11 August 2020

I'm sure when you work in your glass shop you notice a rather curious pattern repeating itself along the edge of the windscreen and the car windows. You may have seen a band of black specks repeated thousands of times on any type of vehicle window, whether it's a car, bus or any other means of public transport. But what is their function?

Windscreens and windows are an engineering marvel and part of that engineering involves how the glass adheres to the frame and what do those black bands topped with black dots have to do with it.

IFirst of all, those black bands are called frits and they are made of a type of hyper-resistant ceramic paint. It generally serves three purposes: firstly, it is a coating that protects the urethane insulation adhesive underneath from UVA rays. Secondly, the frit is what keeps the windshield and windows in place and finally it serves an aesthetic function: to hide the adhesive.

The functionality of these black strips is, in a nutshell, to provide a surface to secure the glass to the frame of the car. However, there is a double purpose: while the side of the frit facing the inside allows the windows to be sealed to the vehicle, the side facing the outside acts as a shield against ultraviolet rays to protect that bond. Otherwise, the adhesive would be weakened by continuous exposure to sunlight.

Why are the spots black?

The specks that finish the black band are a pattern of dots that draw a kind of gradient. Their function is aesthetic, as they provide a more visually attractive, fluid and pleasing transition from the black band to the clear glass. However, they also have other very interesting functionalities. Among them, the speckles serve to better distribute the temperature and to sift the sunlight: they allow to reduce the flash of sunlight that the driver and passenger visors cannot block.
